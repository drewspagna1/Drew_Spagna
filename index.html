About

I left oil and gas years ago to get in early on something new. RPA was barely a category yet, most companies didn't know what to call it, and that was exactly what pulled me in. I've kept chasing that same feeling ever since, getting to a shift before it's obvious and figuring out what it actually means in practice.

Over the years I've deliberately worked every angle of this. Pre-sales solutions consulting, strategy and advisory work, post-sales solutions architecture, I've sat on pretty much every side of the table in enterprise technology, and I did that on purpose. That cross-functional range is probably the thing I bring that's hardest to fake: I can sell the vision, scope it, build it, and then stick around to make sure it actually works once it's live.

I don't think of myself as an "automation person," even though a lot of my resume reads that way. What I actually care about is figuring out where AI and modern systems architecture can change how a business runs, then making that real, whether that's agentic workflows, LLM orchestration, data and integration architecture, or something else entirely. Ten plus years in and the core skill hasn't changed: understand a messy problem well enough to explain it back to the people who own it, then build something that holds up in production. I like the parts of this work most people skip, the debugging, the edge cases, the documentation that makes a handoff actually work.

Agentic Workflow Automation

Durable orchestration pipeline for AI-driven work processing

I didn't build this orchestration framework from scratch, I inherited it and pushed it a lot further. The core pattern processes work items end to end without a person babysitting every step: a durable orchestrator kicks off a get-work stage, chains into a second activity that loads validated items onto a queue, and a separate queue-triggered function actually does the work asynchronously, so one slow item can't block the whole batch. My contribution was taking that framework and building a standardized, object-oriented orchestration layer on top of it, as an optional path teams could opt into instead of rebuilding the same pipeline logic by hand for every new customer. Nobody was forced onto it, but it was there so a team could apply the blueprint and adapt just the pieces specific to that customer's process instead of starting from a blank file.
